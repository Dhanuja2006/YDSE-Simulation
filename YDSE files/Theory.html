YOUNG'S DOUBLE SLIT EXPERIMENT






THEORY:


Young's double slit experiment is fundamendamental experiment in the feild of physics that provides storong evidence for wave nature of light. The experiment demonstrates the phenomenon of interference, which occurs when waves overlap and interact with each other.

Young's double-slit experiment typically involves a barrier with two narrow slits (the double slit) through which light or other waves can pass. Behind the slits is a screen that records the pattern formed by the waves.

A source of monochromatic light, such as a laser, is used to illuminate the double slit. Monochromatic light consists of a single wavelength, which is essential for this experiment. When the monochromatic light passes through the double slit, it behaves like a wave. The waves emanating from the two slits overlap and interfere with each other as they propagate to the screen. This interference produces a distinctive pattern of alternating light and dark regions on the screen.The resulting pattern on the screen shows alternating bright and dark bands, known as interference fringes. The bright bands correspond to constructive interference, where the waves from both slits reinforce each other, creating a brighter spot. The dark bands represent destructive interference, where the waves from the two slits cancel each other out, creating a darker spot.The interference pattern can only be explained by the wave nature of light. If light behaved solely as particles (photons), you would not see this characteristic pattern.

CONCLUSION:


This experiment played a crucial role in the development of the wave theory of light and the confirmation of the wave-particle duality of light, where light exhibits both wave-like and particle-like behavior depending on how it is observed.
